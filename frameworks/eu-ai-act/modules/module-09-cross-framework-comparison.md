# Module 9 — Comparing EU AI Act, ISO 42001, and NIST AI RMF

## Why this module is different from everything before it

Every prior module in this curriculum — across all three frameworks —
introduced new substantive content. This module doesn't. It sits across
everything already built and asks how the pieces actually relate to each
other. This is a genuinely distinct and valuable skill in its own right:
a client rarely asks about a single framework in isolation. They usually
ask some version of "which of these do we actually need, and do they
overlap, or are we paying for the same work three times?" Being able to
answer that precisely, framework by framework and clause by article, is
exactly what separates a GRC engineer who has memorized three separate
curricula from one who can actually advise a client on how to combine
them efficiently.

## The three frameworks answer three different questions

The most important thing to internalize is that these frameworks are not
competitors offering alternative paths to the same outcome. They answer
genuinely different questions, and an organization can need all three
simultaneously without any of the work being redundant.

**NIST AI RMF answers: how do we think about AI risk well?** It's
voluntary guidance with no enforcement mechanism, structured around four
functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — that build organizational AI
risk maturity from the ground up. It's the lightest-weight and most
flexible of the three frameworks, which is exactly why it's often the
right starting point for an organization with low existing AI governance
maturity.

**ISO 42001 answers: how do we prove to a third party that we think
about it well, in a structured, auditable way?** It's a voluntary,
certifiable management system standard built around ten clauses and the
PDCA cycle, with a genuine external certification process — accreditation
bodies, stage 1 and stage 2 audits, a three-year surveillance cycle —
that produces a credential clients and partners can independently verify.

**The EU AI Act answers: what are we legally required to do, regardless
of whether we want to prove anything to anyone?** It's binding law with
real enforcement bodies and financial penalties, structured around four
risk tiers where obligations are entirely conditional on classification
rather than applying uniformly the way NIST AI RMF's guidance and ISO
42001's clauses do.

A company building AI products with EU market exposure doesn't choose
between these three. It likely needs NIST AI RMF's conceptual scaffolding
to actually think clearly about AI risk in the first place, ISO 42001
certification because clients and partners want independently verified
proof of governance maturity, and EU AI Act compliance because the law
applies whether the organization ever chooses to engage with it or not.

## Where the substantive content genuinely overlaps

The structural differences above are real, but the more practically
useful question for engagement efficiency is where the actual content —
not just the framework's nature — overlaps.

**Risk assessment and treatment.** ISO 42001's Clause 6 risk assessment,
covered in Module 5 of that curriculum, and the EU AI Act's Article 9
risk management system, covered in Module 6 of this curriculum, are doing
genuinely similar analytical work: identifying risks, estimating
likelihood and impact, selecting and implementing mitigation, and
tracking residual risk. An organization that has already built a rigorous
ISO 42001 risk methodology has done most of the conceptual heavy lifting
Article 9 requires. It still needs EU AI Act-specific adaptations — the
explicit reasonably-foreseeable-misuse framing, the named attention to
vulnerable groups including children — but it is not starting from zero.

**Organizational foundation and leadership commitment.** NIST AI RMF's
Govern function and ISO 42001's Clauses 4 and 5 (context of the
organization and leadership, covered in Module 2 of the ISO 42001
curriculum) cover almost identical organizational groundwork: understanding
context and stakeholders, establishing policy, defining roles and
accountability. Work done to satisfy one substantially informs the other.

**Data governance and human oversight.** ISO 42001's Annex A controls
around data quality (covered in Module 3 of that curriculum) and the EU
AI Act's Article 10 data governance requirements (Module 7 of this
curriculum) are asking related questions about training data quality and
bias, even though the EU AI Act's standards are legally specific in ways
ISO 42001's controls catalog leaves more open to an organization's own
risk-based judgment.

This overlap is exactly what makes a module-by-module approach across all
three frameworks valuable as a coherent service offering rather than
three disconnected products. A real client engagement can genuinely reuse
foundational work across frameworks instead of rebuilding the same
analysis three separate times.

## Where they diverge sharply — and why pretending otherwise is a mistake

It would be a disservice to a client to imply the overlap above is total.
Several areas have no real equivalent across frameworks, and being
precise about this prevents a client from being caught off guard later.

**ISO 42001's certification process has no equivalent in the EU AI
Act.** Module 4 of the ISO 42001 curriculum covered accreditation bodies,
stage 1 and stage 2 audits, and a three-year surveillance cycle — all
voluntary. Module 4 of this EU AI Act curriculum covered conformity
assessment, which sounds structurally similar but is a legally mandatory
pre-market gate, not a voluntary third-party certification an
organization opts into for commercial reasons.

**The EU AI Act's risk-tier classification system has no equivalent in
either prior framework.** Neither NIST AI RMF nor ISO 42001 conditions
its obligations on a system's classification the way the EU AI Act does.
ISO 42001's clauses apply uniformly to every certified organization
regardless of what specific AI it builds; the EU AI Act's obligations
are entirely dependent on which of the four tiers from Module 1 a given
system falls into.

**The EU AI Act's public transparency mechanism — the EU database from
Module 4 — has no parallel in either prior framework.** Both ISO 42001's
audit evidence and NIST AI RMF's documentation remain internal to the
organization, or visible at most to a certification body. The EU AI Act
creates public visibility into high-risk system registrations that
neither voluntary framework requires or even contemplates.

**NIST AI RMF has no certification mechanism and no legal force at
all**, distinguishing it from both other frameworks in opposite
directions — lighter than ISO 42001's voluntary certification, and
entirely absent the legal weight of the EU AI Act.

## What this means for BridgeCore's service offering

This comparison is the foundation of a genuinely differentiated
consulting capability: cross-framework gap analysis. A client who is
already ISO 42001 certified does not need EU AI Act compliance work built
from scratch — they need a precise gap analysis identifying exactly what
their existing ISO 42001 work already covers (much of Clause 6's risk
methodology, much of Clauses 4 and 5's organizational foundation), what
needs EU AI Act-specific extension (the foreseeable-misuse framing in
Article 9, the vulnerable-groups consideration), and what is entirely new
work with no ISO 42001 equivalent at all (classification under Annex III,
conformity assessment, EU database registration).

This is a higher-value, more efficient engagement than treating each
framework as an independent project requiring its own assessment from
zero, and it is only possible because of the kind of detailed,
module-by-module understanding this curriculum has built across all
three frameworks.

## Key terms

| Term | Plain meaning |
|---|---|
| Cross-framework gap analysis | Identifying what existing compliance work in one framework already satisfies, partially satisfies, or doesn't address in another |
| Substantive overlap | Areas where two frameworks require genuinely similar analytical work, even if their legal status differs |
| Structural divergence | Areas where one framework has a mechanism (e.g., certification, public registration) with no equivalent in another |

## How to explain it in an interview

> "The way I think about these three frameworks is that they answer
> different questions rather than compete with each other. NIST AI RMF
> is about how to think about AI risk well — voluntary, flexible, no
> enforcement. ISO 42001 is about proving you think about it well to a
> third party — voluntary but certifiable, with real external audit
> mechanics. The EU AI Act is about what you're legally required to do
> regardless of whether you want to prove anything — binding law, with
> obligations that depend entirely on risk classification rather than
> applying uniformly. Where it gets practically useful is the overlap:
> ISO 42001's Clause 6 risk assessment and the EU AI Act's Article 9 risk
> management system are doing genuinely similar analytical work, so an
> organization with mature ISO 42001 risk processes isn't starting from
> zero on EU AI Act compliance. But there are real gaps too — ISO 42001's
> whole certification apparatus has no EU AI Act equivalent, and the EU
> AI Act's public database registration has no equivalent in either
> voluntary framework. Being precise about where the overlap is real and
> where it isn't is what makes cross-framework gap analysis a genuinely
> valuable service rather than just running three separate assessments."
